The Myth of 'Flyover Country'

'Flyover country' is a phrase derisively used to refer to the midwestern states of the U.S. It implies that there is little of value in these states, few tourist attractions or points of interest.
But how true is that? Do people really fly from coast to coast avoiding these huge swathes of land?
Let's examine the data to see if there are noticable differences between the flight habits of those on the coasts and the people in 'flyover country'.

Does the midwest have fewer airports than the rest of the country?

The U.S Census Bureau defines the American mid west as consisting of the following states:
Illinois, Indiana, Iowa, Kansas, Michigan, Minnesota, Missouri, Nebraska, North Dakota, Ohio, South Dakota, and Wisconsin.
On the right, we see that when it comes to number of airports per state, the midwest states are comfortably in the middle.
While they don't have the most airports, they don't have the fewest either.

It's not just about number of airports

We see on the right that there is a kernel of truth to the label 'flyover country'. Despite having a middling number of airports, most of those airports are quite small, that is to say, they are used less frequently than airports in other states.
It is plain to see that most frequently used airports in the U.S appear around its eastern and western coasts.

How about flight paths?

Similar to the previous section, the flight paths most frequently used may be on the coasts, but the midwest still has some high achievers.
